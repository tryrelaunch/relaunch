What BYOB Means Here

What "BYOB" actually means on Malarky.

"BYOB" on Malarky means almost exactly what it sounds like. You bring the beer, the wine, the champagne, the spirits, the cocktail mixers, the non-alcoholic drinks — whatever your group actually wants to drink for the duration of the charter. There's no quantity cap within reason. There's no required minimum. There's no bar staff suggesting you order more.

The reason this works on Malarky and doesn't work on most other San Diego charter boats is structural, not philosophical. Most charter vessels in San Diego hold a liquor license. A liquor license requires the licensed business to be the sole alcohol provider on board, which is what enables the bar-tab model that funds those boats. Malarky doesn't hold a liquor license — we made the structural choice to skip it — and that's what enables you to bring your own.

You also get a fully private yacht charter on San Diego Bay in the bargain. The whole 47-foot catamaran is yours for the charter window, not a section of it. No other groups sharing the deck. No tour guide narrating landmarks. Whatever drinks you brought, you brought for your group alone.

Captain and crew don't drink during charters. Federal regulation: they're operating a U.S. Coast Guard-certified vessel and the safety of the boat is their job. So when you're calculating how much to bring, you're calculating for your guests — not for the crew — and the captain is your sober operator from dock to dock.

The Two Rules

Two rules: no glass, no red wine.

BYOB on Malarky has exactly two restrictions, each in place for a specific reason.

No glass containers. Anything you bring in glass — wine bottles, champagne, spirits, sparkling water, kombucha — gets transferred to plastic cups, pitchers, or bottles by our crew at the dock before boarding. Two reasons: safety (glass that breaks on a fiberglass deck is hard to fully clean up, and bare feet are common on a yacht charter); and environmental (a dropped glass bottle goes to the bottom of San Diego Bay and stays there). The dock-side transfer takes about two minutes per group, and our crew has done it a thousand times.

No red wine. Red wine permanently stains the white deck and the cream upholstery on Malarky. Pigment soaks into the fiberglass and the fabric within seconds, and even immediate cleaning doesn't fully lift it. White wine, rosé, champagne, prosecco, sparkling rosé, and any other sparkling wine are all welcome. If you must have a bold red at some point in the evening, save it for the post-charter dinner.

Pro tip from regulars: a lot of returning guests skip the dock-side transfer entirely by pre-decanting at home. Pick up a 12-pack of empty plastic water bottles at the grocery store, decant your bottles before you head to Shelter Island, label the bottles with masking tape, and you'll save a few minutes at the dock and avoid carrying glass at all.

The Math

Why BYOB saves real money.

The savings on BYOB compared to a bar-tab charter aren't marginal — they're often the difference between an extra hundred dollars and an extra thousand. Here's the math, by item.

On a typical bar-tab charter (the licensed-bar model used by most yacht-rental boats in San Diego), expect roughly: $8.50 for a domestic draft beer, $10.50 for a mixed drink, $13 for a specialty cocktail, and $70–$90 for a bottle of mid-tier champagne. Those prices are reasonable for a licensed bar — they fund the liquor license, the bartender's wages, and the inventory carry — but they add up quickly across a 12-guest group and a 3-hour window.

Compare to what the same drinks cost at Costco, Trader Joe's, or Total Wine the day before your charter:

A bottle of champagne — $20 at Costco vs $80 on a bar tab. A case of beer — $25 at retail vs roughly $200 in draft tabs across 12 guests over 3 hours (assuming each guest has 2 beers). A bottle of vodka — $25 at retail vs $130 in mixed-drink tabs. Mixers (tonic, soda, juice) — $15 for the lot at the grocery store vs $4–$6 each on a bar tab.

A worked example. A 12-guest 3-hour charter where everyone wants three drinks. On a bar-tab boat: 36 mixed drinks at $10.50 = $378, plus a couple of bottles of champagne for toasts = $160, plus snacks/water markup = $50. Total bar bill: roughly $590 plus tip. On Malarky BYOB: a Costco run of 1.5 cases of beer ($40), 4 bottles of champagne ($80), a bottle of vodka ($25), a 4-pack of mixers ($20), a 24-pack of water ($5), a tray of charcuterie ($60). Total cost: $230, no tip on alcohol.

Realistic savings on a typical charter: $400 to $800 per outing, with the high end at sunset-charter and bachelorette pricing. The savings compound by guest, by hour, and by drinking pace.

Packing Guide

What to bring, how to pack it.

Coolers and ice. We provide one cooler stocked with ice for every charter — that's the baseline. If your group is bringing a lot of drinks, bring one or two extra coolers and we'll store them on the aft deck, restocking from the boat's ice supply as needed. You don't need to bring your own ice. We have it.

What formats work best. Cans for beer, hard seltzer, mixed drinks, and sparkling water. Plastic bottles or boxed wine for white, rosé, and sparkling (or decanted from glass at the dock). Plastic-decanted spirits if you're bringing liquor. Cartons or plastic bottles for mixers, juice, and soda. Anything in glass gets transferred at the dock — not a problem, just a couple of extra minutes.

Recommended quantities for 12 guests over 3–4 hours. A useful starting point: 1.5 cases of beer (36 cans), 4–6 bottles of wine or champagne, 1 bottle of spirits plus 4–6 mixers if you're making cocktails, one 24-pack of water minimum, and 1 case of non-alcoholic mixers or sparkling. Most groups under-bring; you can always leave with leftovers.

Where to shop. Costco is the consensus pick — pricing on champagne, beer, and snacks is the cleanest in San Diego. Trader Joe's for wine variety. Total Wine for spirits. Vons or Ralphs for last-minute fills. The Costco closest to Shelter Island is the Morena Boulevard location; budget about 45 minutes for a stock-up run including parking and check-out.

Snack-side packing. Pre-packaged charcuterie boards, sliced fruit, chips and dip, and a few sweet options travel well and require zero on-boat prep. Skip anything that needs heating or refrigeration beyond the cooler.

BYOF Too

Bring your own food, or we can arrange catering.

The same logic that drives BYOB drives BYOF. You bring the food. Most groups bring takeout from a favorite spot in town — sushi platters, sandwich trays, charcuterie boards, taco spreads, pizza — or grocery-store assemblies that travel well. We don't cook onboard, but our crew is happy to plate, serve, and clean up whatever you bring. Hot food in non-glass containers is fine; just plan on eating it within the first hour or two before it cools.

If you'd rather not handle the food side at all, we partner with a roster of local San Diego caterers who can deliver to the dock or board with your group. Options range from light bites and charcuterie to fully plated multi-course meals, with menu and pricing varied by group size and dietary needs. Mention catering when you book and we'll send options. See the homepage FAQ on catering for the short version.

The same two beverage rules apply to anything you bring food-side — no glass containers (including kombucha or fancy sparkling juice), and no red wine in any food-pairing form.

Safety Boundary

Captain & crew — where the line is.

BYOB on Malarky is permissive with what you bring. It's not permissive with what happens after you've had too much of it. The line is clearly drawn and the captain is the one drawing it.

Captain and crew don't drink during charters. Federal regulation: anyone operating a USCG-certified vessel must be sober. That's not a Malarky policy — that's the law. So your captain is your sober operator for the full charter, watching the boat, the route, the conditions, and the guests.

Captain has full authority on safety. In rare cases — we mean rare, maybe once a season — the captain will refuse a clearly intoxicated guest at the dock before departure. We never embarrass anyone, we never make a scene, but if someone can't safely board, they don't board. The rest of the group sails. The vast majority of charters never see this come up.

The no-glass-overboard rule is strictly enforced. If you've decanted glass to plastic and we still see a glass container on board, it goes back to the dock. Same for anything that ends up overboard — we don't move past it; we recover what we can and we don't accept the line of "it was an accident." A few minutes of care from everyone keeps the bay clean.
