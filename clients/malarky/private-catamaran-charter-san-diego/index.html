Why a Catamaran

Why a catamaran for a private charter.

Two hulls instead of one. The boat sits flat instead of heeling, so drinks stay where you put them and guests in heels don't have to brace against the rail. A 47-foot catamaran has the deck space of a much longer monohull: four staterooms with doors that close, two real bathrooms, an indoor lounge that doesn't feel like a converted boat, and three distinct outdoor zones across the bow and aft. Shallow draft means the captain can position close to the Coronado Bridge, the Embarcadero, and the Midway without staying out in the deep channel. For the broader picture of how a private yacht charter on Malarky comes together, see that page.

The Vessel

The Voyage 47, pedigree without the open ocean.

Malarky is a Voyage 47, built in Cape Town, South Africa by a shipyard that turns out blue-water voyaging catamarans. The hull, hardware, and safety systems were engineered for ocean passages, meaning the build quality exceeds what most charter boats this size carry. Heavier rigging, better-rated chainplates, sealed deck hardware, the kind of structural attention that's only economic when the boat needs to survive thousand-mile crossings.

We sail Malarky primarily on San Diego Bay. The blue-water pedigree translates here as comfort and stability, the hull behaves the same way it would on a passage, just on flat water with no weather to manage. The result is a yacht that operates well above the standard for sheltered-water charters: minimal roll, smooth sailing in afternoon breeze, near-silent operation when the engines aren't running. It's also the foundation of why Malarky reads as a real luxury yacht rental in San Diego, not a stretched-up day charter.

The Boat, Up Close

Three places to be on the boat.

The indoor lounge is the social heart, comfortable seating around a coffee table, large bay-side windows, a stocked mini fridge for the BYOB cold drinks, modern decor that doesn't feel like a charter rental. The aft deck is the outdoor living room with shaded table seating for eight, where most meals happen and where steps lead down to the swim platform during anchor stops. Forward of the mast, the bow trampoline net stretches between the hulls, guests lie out on it during the cruise, watch the bow wake, and frequently spot dolphins pacing the boat. Across a 3-4 hour day most groups use all three.

And on Malarky specifically, you get a 47-foot sailing catamaran, not a motor yacht, meaning quieter, more authentic, and a genuine sailing experience when conditions allow.
