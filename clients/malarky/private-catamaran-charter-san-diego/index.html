Why a Catamaran

Twin hulls change everything.

A catamaran has two hulls instead of one, and that single fact changes nearly every aspect of the charter experience. The most immediate difference is stability. Catamarans don't heel — they don't tip sideways under sail or in a chop the way monohulls do. Guests who get seasick on traditional sailboats often have no issue on Malarky; drinks stay on tables, plates stay on laps, guests in heels don't have to brace against the rail.

The second difference is deck space. A 47-foot catamaran has materially more deck area than a 47-foot monohull. Twelve guests would feel crowded on a monohull that long; on Malarky they spread out across three distinct zones — indoor lounge, aft deck, bow trampoline — without crowding any of them. Real conversational privacy is possible even with a full party aboard, which is part of what makes the standard private yacht charter on Malarky feel different from a same-length monohull.

The third is cabin volume. Twin hulls give you four real staterooms and two real bathrooms in a length that on a monohull would barely fit two cabins and one head. The interior doesn't feel like a converted boat; it feels like a small modern apartment.

The fourth is draft. Catamarans sit shallow in the water, which means the captain can position closer to the landmarks that matter — the Coronado Bridge, the Embarcadero, the Midway — without staying out in the deep channel.

The Vessel

The Voyage 47 — pedigree without the open ocean.

Malarky is a Voyage 47, built in Cape Town, South Africa by a shipyard that turns out blue-water voyaging catamarans. The hull, hardware, and safety systems were engineered for ocean passages — meaning the build quality exceeds what most charter boats this size carry. Heavier rigging, better-rated chainplates, sealed deck hardware, the kind of structural attention that's only economic when the boat needs to survive thousand-mile crossings.

We sail Malarky exclusively on San Diego Bay. The blue-water pedigree translates here as comfort and stability — the hull behaves the same way it would on a passage, just on flat water with no weather to manage. The result is a yacht that operates well above the standard for sheltered-water charters: minimal roll, smooth sailing in afternoon breeze, near-silent operation when the engines aren't running. It's also the foundation of why Malarky reads as a real luxury yacht rental in San Diego, not a stretched-up day charter.

We do not run open-ocean or offshore charters. The pedigree is a feature; the operating envelope is the bay. Open-ocean charters require different safety equipment, different captain credentials, different insurance, and a charter window measured in days rather than hours — none of which suit the typical Malarky guest. San Diego Bay is also genuinely the best charter water in the region: protected, photogenic, packed with landmarks, and reliably wildlife-active. The bay doesn't need to be left.

Forty-seven feet is meaningful length. The staterooms are real private rooms with doors that close. The lounge is a proper lounge. The bathrooms are working heads with showers. Voyage built a yacht; we operate it as one.

Sailing, Not Power

Why sailing, not power.

This is the differentiator nobody else in our charter family can match. Triton, Athena, Alana, and Aquata are all power vessels. They have their virtues — speed, weather independence, simplicity for guests who don't care how they get to the destination. But none of them sail. Malarky is the only sailing catamaran in the family.

What sailing changes for the charter experience: the engines come off when conditions permit and the boat operates on wind alone. The sound shifts — what was diesel hum becomes the sound of wind in the rigging, water against the hulls, the soft pop of sails filling. The pace shifts — sailing is quieter and less mechanical than motoring, which lets conversations slow down too. The whole platform feels more authentic, more like the kind of yachting experience that's getting harder to find on a charter rental.

When wind doesn't cooperate, the captain motors. You don't have to choose in advance: the captain reads the day and uses whatever the conditions favor. Most charter days end up doing some of both — motor out of the harbor, sails up once you've cleared the wind shadow, motor back when the breeze drops at sunset. The transitions are seamless.

Three Spaces

Three distinct spaces, one 47-foot yacht.

The catamaran's layout gives Malarky three genuinely different charter zones, and across a 3–4 hour day most groups use all of them.

The indoor lounge is the interior social zone. Comfortable seating arranged around a coffee table, large bay-side windows that flood the space with natural light, a modern decor approach that doesn't feel like a charter rental, and a stocked mini fridge to keep BYOB cold throughout the day. It's the rest spot when guests want shade or a quieter moment indoors. On hot summer afternoons or cooler evening charters, the lounge becomes the social center of the boat — where the toasts happen, the food gets passed, the conversations slow down.

The aft deck is the exterior living room. A shaded covered table with seating for around eight. The canopy stays in place against the San Diego sun. Most meals and most easy lounging happen here — the view forward is the bay and the city, the view aft is the wake. Steps lead down to the swim platform from this deck for water access during anchor stops.

The bow trampoline is the boat's signature. A wide net stretched between the hulls forward of the mast, low over the water. Guests lie out on the trampoline during the cruise, watching the bow wake and the dolphins that frequently pace alongside. It's the photo location on every charter that has a photographer aboard, and the “lay out and feel the wind” spot for everyone else.

Pricing & how to book.

Private catamaran charters on Malarky are quote-based. Rates vary by season, time of day (sunset slots run higher), group size, and any add-ons you've included. We'll send a clean quote within a few hours of your inquiry — no surprises, no padding.

Booking minimums and cancellation.

The minimum charter length is two hours. Most charters book three to four hours, which we recommend — long enough to take in the bay under sail, anchor for a swim, deploy any add-ons, and return without rushing. Two hours feels compressed by the time you've boarded and the captain's set sail.

Cancellation policy. Full deposit refund three or more weeks before your charter. Free reschedule up to one week before departure. Within one week of departure, the deposit becomes non-refundable.
