Micro Wedding & Elopement

What a wedding on a yacht actually looks like.

A micro wedding on Malarky runs four to twelve guests including the couple. Elopements typically run two to six. The ceremony happens on the bow trampoline at golden hour — the wide net between the hulls becomes the aisle and the altar, with the Coronado Bridge as the western horizon and the downtown skyline lighting up behind the wedding party. Officiant stands at the bow point; the couple faces aft; guests sit on the trampoline or along the bow rails. The ceremony itself takes ten to fifteen minutes.

The reception happens on the shaded aft deck. Table seating for around eight, room to stand and mingle for the full party. This is where the first toasts happen, the catered meal lands (or your BYO charcuterie spread, depending on how you've planned the food), and the conversation slows down. The aft deck looks west toward the bridge — meaning the reception faces the same sunset that anchored the ceremony.

The indoor lounge is where the dance moment happens (the boat has a sound system; bring a Spotify playlist), where the bridal party can step away from the deck for a quiet moment, and where the bay still photographs cleanly through the windows. Modern decor, natural light through the bay-side glass, a coffee table for the cake or the dessert.

Why couples pick this instead of a venue: no rental fees, no "venue minimum" food costs, no setup-strike day-of, no fluorescent ballroom. The wedding is the boat is the bay. You and twelve people who matter, on a 47-foot catamaran, on the prettiest stretch of San Diego Bay at the best time of day. The setting does the work.

Three Spaces

Three wedding spaces on one 47-foot yacht.

Most yacht charters give you one space. Malarky's private catamaran charter gives you three, and a wedding day is the one occasion that uses every one of them.

The bow trampoline — the ceremony location. A wide net stretched between the hulls forward of the mast. Photographers describe it as the cleanest wedding-ceremony foreground in the San Diego charter fleet: nothing between the couple and the bay except open net and sky. The Coronado Bridge frames the western horizon. The sun drops behind the bridge during the ceremony if you've timed it right.

The aft deck — the reception space. Shaded covered table seating for around eight, room to stand and mingle for the full twelve-guest party. This is where dinner happens, where the toasts are made, where most of the easy lounging unfolds. The canopy stays in place during summer ceremonies for sun protection; comes off in cooler months if your group wants direct light.

The indoor lounge — the dance, the photo, and the refresh. Comfortable seating around a coffee table, large bay-side windows, a stocked mini fridge, modern decor that photographs well. The first dance happens here in colder months when the deck cools after sunset. The bridal-party getting-ready photo happens here in summer. The lounge is the "step inside for a moment" space when the deck gets crowded or the couple wants a private minute.

Wedding-Day Logistics

Timeline — how the day unfolds.

A typical three-hour micro-wedding charter runs roughly like this. Adjustments are normal — if your group wants a longer reception, book four hours instead.

T-minus 90 minutes from sunset — boarding and cocktail hour. The wedding party arrives at the Shelter Island dock, the crew handles boarding and lifejacket placement, drinks come out (champagne is conventional — you brought it), light snacks if you've planned a cocktail-hour spread. The boat departs the harbor within the first 15 minutes.

T-minus 60 to 15 minutes — cruise to ceremony position. The captain positions toward the Coronado Bridge for the ceremony angle, holding course or slowing as needed. This is the time when most of the pre-ceremony photos happen — couple shots on the bow, group photos with the bay backdrop, the getting-ready candids in the lounge if the couple is doing a final look.

T-minus 15 to 0 — ceremony at peak golden hour. Wedding party assembles on the bow trampoline. Officiant takes position. The ceremony itself runs about ten to fifteen minutes (vows, ring exchange, kiss, pronounced moment). The captain holds course for the photo angle and pace.

Post-ceremony to blue hour — reception. The couple moves to the aft deck, the meal or charcuterie comes out, the toasts happen, the first dance if you're doing one (move to the indoor lounge once the air cools). The boat continues a slow cruise around the bay, returning to the dock during the blue-hour quiet afterward.

Coordinate with us at booking on: ceremony timing (we'll calculate exact sunset for your date), officiant logistics (you bring; we can recommend), music and sound (we have the system; bring a playlist or a Bluetooth-connected device), and any decor you want on the aft table (florals work well; bow stays photogenic-clean).

The Setting

Why sunset weddings work here.

Sunset weddings on San Diego Bay are visually unmatched in Southern California. The Coronado Bridge stretched against the western sky is the frame; the Point Loma silhouette is what shapes the western horizon; the downtown skyline starts to light up behind the ceremony in the back half. The color progression is reliable: amber as the sun drops toward the bridge, pink at the peak ten to fifteen minutes, deep teal afterward, then the blue hour quiet on the return cruise.

Plan the ceremony at the peak — typically 15 minutes before official sunset time. We calculate exact timing for your date when you book. Winter sunsets (4:45pm in December) mean afternoon ceremonies and earlier dinners. Summer sunsets (8:00pm in late June) mean evening ceremonies and reception food served on the dock-out leg of the cruise. Spring and fall have the most flexible windows and arguably the clearest skies of the year — October sunsets are a photographer's dream.

For couples who want the sunset as the centerpiece of the day but don't need a full wedding ceremony aboard, see our private sunset yacht charter page — same boat, same bay, lighter format. Both products year-round.

Photography

Photography considerations.

The bay at golden hour is what wedding photographers consistently describe as one of the best on-water settings in Southern California. The backdrop does most of the work; the photographer just has to be present, ready, and not let the light slip. Three things help.

Use the three spaces deliberately. Bow trampoline for the ceremony itself and the immediate post-ceremony couple portraits — this is the photo every officiant dreams of taking. Aft deck for reception candids, toasts, and the group-around-the-table shot. The indoor lounge for the getting-ready frames or first-dance shots if you're doing them.

Brief your photographer ahead of the day. If they haven't shot a Malarky wedding before, send them a quick rundown of the three spaces and the timing pattern. The bow trampoline ceremony angle is the one most photographers underutilize on their first charter — they don't realize they can shoot from the helm deck looking forward for the wide composition.

Bring backup batteries. Wind near the water and the cooler evening air drain batteries faster than studio conditions. Two backups per camera body. Cards too — the bay is photogenic for a reason and you'll fill cards faster than expected.

We can recommend local San Diego photographers who've worked Malarky weddings before. They know the light, the angles, and the rhythm of the day. Ask at booking and we'll send a short list.

Food & Drink

Catering, champagne, and the BYOB wedding.

Two paths on the food-and-drink side, both popular and both fully workable.

Catered. We partner with a roster of local San Diego caterers and can arrange anything from cocktail-style light bites to plated multi-course meals. Mention catering when you book and we'll send menus and pricing options that fit your guest count, time of day, and dietary needs. The crew handles plate, serve, and clear — caterers drop and we run the service from there. Plated dinners work cleanly for 8 to 12 guests on the aft deck.

BYO. Pick up takeout from a favorite San Diego restaurant, assemble a charcuterie spread, order a dessert from a local bakery. Works just as well for an intimate wedding as a catered dinner, often runs a fraction of the cost, and pairs naturally with the relaxed-but-real vibe of a 12-guest wedding. Crew handles plate, serve, and clear regardless of who provided the food.

Champagne is the rule, not the exception. Bring as much as you want — this is what BYOB privileges are for. A bottle for the ceremony toast, a few more for the reception, sparkling rosé for the dance moment. Malarky is one of the only yacht charters in San Diego that allows BYOB; see full BYOB charter details for the rules. Two of them: no glass containers (we transfer to plastic cups or pitchers before boarding — takes about two minutes) and no red wine (it permanently stains the white deck). White wine, rosé, champagne, sparkling, beer, cocktails-in-cans, premium spirits decanted to plastic — all welcome. Bring what your wedding actually wants to drink.

Larger Weddings

When to choose Triton instead — large weddings.

Malarky maxes at twelve guests by design and by U.S. Coast Guard rule. If your wedding is planning for thirty, sixty, or a hundred guests, this is the wrong boat — and we'd rather tell you that up front than try to make Malarky work for the wrong wedding.

For larger weddings, our sister vessel Triton Charters is the right call. Triton is a 75-foot catamaran, USCG-certified for up to 100 guests, with a built-in bar (and bartender service), a dance floor, and a full crew. Same San Diego Bay, same captain-quality and crew-hospitality model, completely different scale. Triton runs both ticketed-style and fully-private wedding charters in the 30-to-100-guest range — that's the boat for receptions, larger ceremonies, and any wedding where the guest list runs past Malarky's cap.

Malarky is the right call when you specifically want intimate, twelve-guest, on-the-water. Triton is the right call when you specifically want larger, ceremony-or-reception venue-on-the-water at scale. Different boats for different weddings.

Pricing & Booking

Pricing & how to book.

Wedding charters on Malarky are quote-based. Rates vary by season, weekend vs weekday (weekend evenings run higher), charter length, and any catering or add-ons you've arranged through us. We'll send a clean quote within a few hours of your inquiry — no padding, no upcharge on items you didn't ask about.

For the broader structure of how charter pricing works on Malarky — hourly blocks, seasonal variance, sunset premiums — see pricing details.

Length and timing.

Most micro weddings charter for three hours — the sweet spot for cocktail hour + ceremony at golden hour + reception meal + blue-hour return. Couples planning longer receptions book four hours. Elopements with very small parties (2–6 guests) sometimes book the two-hour minimum, which gives time for ceremony + champagne + photos and a return cruise.

Booking window.

Weekend evenings in summer book four to eight weeks ahead. Spring and fall are typically two to four weeks ahead. Winter weddings (still beautiful — the bay clears up in winter and the colder air means clearer sunsets) are usually flexible at one to two weeks. If your date is set, book as early as possible — we'd rather lock it in than have you scrambling.

Cancellation policy: full deposit refund three or more weeks before your charter, free reschedule up to one week before departure, non-refundable within one week. Important to know for weddings given how far ahead they typically book. See the full 2026 cancellation policy →
