Why This Beats the Alternatives

Why a private yacht beats every other bachelorette option.

San Diego bachelorette planning starts with a list of options that all look reasonable until you actually price them, photograph them, and try to fit twelve friends around a single experience. Here's the comparison most maids of honor never get to make with real numbers.

Vs the Gaslamp bar-and-club crawl. Crowded, loud, expensive on drinks, photo backdrops that look like every other bachelorette in San Diego that weekend. You won't get a group shot anyone wants printed. Cover charges plus $13 cocktails times twelve guests times five hours pushes well past what a half-day yacht charter costs, and the experience is split across four venues with travel in between. Yacht charter is one location, one continuous experience, no Ubers.

Vs an Airbnb pool day. No captain handling the day, no crew handling the food and drinks, no real San Diego backdrop, and the neighbors complain by hour two on the weekends. Pool-day photos look like every other pool-day photo. Yacht photos do not.

Vs a party bus. You're technically moving, but not anywhere with a view. Traffic on the way to wherever you ended up. Most San Diego bachelorette party-bus reviews describe the same thing: fun for forty minutes, then bored. A bay charter goes somewhere visually worth the trip.

Vs a ticketed harbor cruise. Shared deck with other ticket-buyers, fixed timing, no BYOB allowed on most of the licensed boats. Cheaper per seat than a private charter, but you don't get the privacy, the BYOB savings, or the actual bachelorette experience, you get a bus with a view.

Vs a themed party boat. The right call if your group is thirty-plus people and the bride specifically wants a disco-themed or pink-themed party with a bartender and a dance floor, that's a real product, and our sister vessel covers it (see Section 9). Different product from what this page is selling. Malarky is the intimate premium option, not the bigger party option.

A private yacht charter on Malarky is the answer when the bachelorette is twelve close people who want premium drinks they actually like, the photos you'd hang on a wall, and an afternoon that doesn't feel like every other bachelorette this weekend.

The Money Math

BYOB saves your bachelorette $300–$500 (real math).

The maid of honor usually coordinates the bachelorette, the group splits costs, and the bride doesn't pay. Drink budget is where the math gets ugly fast on the bar-tab models. Here's the actual comparison for a typical 10-guest, 3-hour bachelorette.

On a bar-tab charter (the licensed-bar model, used by most San Diego party-boat alternatives):

  • ~25 cocktails across the group at $12 average = $300
  • 3 to 4 bottles of bachelorette champagne at $80 each = $240–$320
  • Total drinks: $540–$620 plus tip on the bar tab

On Malarky with BYOB:

  • 4 bottles of Costco champagne at $20 = $80
  • 1 bottle of premium spirits (Tito's, Grey Goose, your call) at $25 = $25
  • Mixers and non-alcoholic (tonic, soda, juice, sparkling water for the non-drinkers) for the group = $55
  • 1 case of beer or hard seltzer at retail = $25
  • Total drinks: ~$185, no tip on retail purchases
Net savings: $355–$435 per bachelorette. Often $400–$500 once you factor in the bar-tab tip on the licensed-boat side. That's money that funds a much better dinner, a longer charter, a wedding gift the bride will actually use, or just stays in pocket where the group can decide how to spend it.

The trick: bring premium drinks at retail prices instead of marked up three-to-four times. A bachelorette is the one occasion where this matters most, you're paying for the drinks anyway, the only question is whether $80 goes to one bar-tab bottle or four Costco bottles.

Two simple rules apply: no drinking from glass (pour into the plastic cups we provide) and no red wine (permanently stains the white deck). Champagne, white wine, rosé, sparkling, beer, hard seltzer, premium spirits decanted to plastic, all welcome. Full breakdown on the BYOB charter details page.

Three Zones

Three bachelorette zones on the boat.

Malarky's private catamaran charter gives you three distinct spaces, a layout advantage that most yacht charters this length can't match. For a bachelorette, each zone has a specific job.

The bow trampoline, the selfie zone and the group shot. A wide net stretched between the hulls forward of the mast, low over the water. This is where the group photos happen, the bridal-party hero shot at golden hour with the Coronado Bridge in the background, the individual posed selfies, the "everyone lying down on the trampoline laughing" candid that ends up in the photo dump. The bow is the photo set. Plan to spend real time here once the cruise is underway.

The aft deck, the main hang zone. Shaded covered table seating for around eight, with room for the rest of the group to stand and mingle. Champagne toasts happen here. Lunch or charcuterie lands here. The first toast to the bride and the gift-opening moment (if you're doing one) work cleanly on the aft deck. The canopy stays in place for sun protection in summer; comes off in cooler months for direct light.

The indoor lounge, the touch-up and outfit-change zone. Comfortable seating around a coffee table, large bay-side windows that flood the space with natural light, a stocked mini fridge to keep BYOB cold, modern decor that photographs cleanly. This is where the outfit changes happen (the daytime swimsuit-coverup-to-dressier-evening transition), where the bride steps away for a minute when the deck gets loud, where the makeup gets touched up before the golden-hour photo set. Forty-seven feet of catamaran means real interior space, not a converted galley.

Photo Logistics

How to plan the bachelorette content.

Bachelorettes generate more social-media content than any other charter type we run. The boat is photogenic, the bay is photogenic, the group is dressed for it. A little planning on the photo side, before the charter, not during, goes a long way.

The shots that make the day. Bow trampoline at golden hour is the hero shot, group lying down in formation with the Coronado Bridge framing the western sky behind them. Aft deck is the champagne-pop and candid-lunch shots. The indoor lounge is the outfit moment and the bride-getting-ready candids. These three locations cover ninety percent of what a bachelorette photographer or DIY iPhone setup wants to capture.

Timing. Golden hour, roughly 90 minutes before sunset, is the photo gold. The light is warm, soft, and forgiving. Mid-afternoon photos in direct San Diego sun are harsh and squinty. If your charter window includes the golden-hour stretch, plan to be in your dressier outfits and on the bow for the group shots during that window. The captain can position the boat for the angle.

If you're hiring a photographer. Brief them on the three zones and the timing pattern, and tell them to shoot the bow trampoline from the helm deck looking forward, first-timer photographers often miss that angle. Bring a portable phone charger for them too; wind near the water drains batteries faster than studio shoots.

If it's DIY phones only. The crew will happily take group shots for you, ask, they're used to it. Bring portable chargers for the whole group (phones die fast on the water), and a waterproof phone pouch or two if you're using the water toys. The lily-pad-and-group footage is the bachelorette reel highlight; pick a phone-keeper who's staying on deck.

Activities

Activities that make the day.

The cruise itself is the baseline product. The activities are what turn a 3-hour charter into a 3-hour event.

Water toys. Paddleboards, kayaks, a 15-foot lily pad floating platform, and tower jump pads. The lily pad is the content highlight reel of most bachelorette charters, whole group on the platform, the crew takes the shot, it ends up in the wedding-weekend Instagram dump. Mention which toys you want when you book; we'll have everything inflated and ready when you anchor. See the water toys page for the full lineup.

Sunset positioning. If your charter window includes sunset (most bachelorettes book the late-afternoon-into-sunset slot), the captain positions for golden hour. The Coronado Bridge in the western frame, the downtown skyline lighting up behind you, the colors progression from amber through pink to deep teal. This is the photo set the entire day was building toward. See the private sunset yacht charter page for what a sunset-focused charter looks like.

Anchor swim time. The captain anchors at Coronado Cays, Glorietta Bay, or sheltered zones behind Shelter Island, sheltered water for floating, swimming, lily-pad lounging, and photo time off the swim platform. About 30 minutes from the dock to most anchorages.

Custom decor. Bring banners, sashes, pre-printed party-game cards, custom plastic cups, matching swimsuit cover-ups, the boat is already a strong backdrop, so most groups go light on decor and let the bay do the visual work. What doesn't work: smoke machines (they trigger boat safety alarms), DJ booths (we have a sound system; bring a Spotify playlist instead), large structural props (logistics), or anything that needs adhesive on the varnished surfaces.

Timing & Length

Bachelorette timing and length.

Three to four hours is the bachelorette sweet spot. Two hours feels rushed by the time everyone's boarded, settled in, and gotten through the first round of drinks, not enough day to make the photos worth the planning. Three to four hours gives space for cruise, water toys, lunch, photos, and the slow-down before the dock-back.

Weekend afternoons book earliest. Saturday and Sunday afternoon slots in summer (May through September) go four to eight weeks ahead. If your bachelorette weekend is set on a specific date and you want a prime Saturday afternoon slot, reserve as soon as the date is confirmed. Spring and fall weekends typically run two to four weeks ahead. Weekday charters and winter weekends usually have more flexibility.

Best time of day depends on the vibe. Daytime (noon to 3pm) leans into swim, water toys, day-drinking energy, the active bachelorette who wants the lily-pad photos and the kayak fleet on the water. Late afternoon (3pm to 6pm bleeding into sunset) gives you both daytime activity AND golden-hour photos in the same charter, the most popular slot for bachelorettes who want everything in one day. Sunset-only (5pm to 8pm) is the elegant champagne-and-skyline version, quieter, dressier, photo-focused.

Planning Checklist

What to bring, how to plan the day.

Bachelorette logistics fall apart when they're improvised on the dock. A short checklist saves the maid of honor real stress.

Drinks. Champagne (the bachelorette staple, bring four bottles for a 10-guest 3-hour charter, more for longer). White wine and rosé (two to four bottles). Beer or hard seltzer (one case). Premium spirits if doing cocktails (one bottle plus mixers). Plenty of water (at least one bottle per guest). Quantities scale linearly, see the Section 3 math for the full retail breakdown. No glass, no red wine.

Food. Grocery-store charcuterie spread, sandwiches or wraps, takeout from a favorite San Diego restaurant (Filippi's, Hodad's, Phil's BBQ all travel well), plus a custom cake or cupcakes for the bride. The crew will plate, serve, and clear whatever you bring. If you'd rather have it catered, we partner with local San Diego caterers, mention catering when you book and we'll send menu options.

Outfits. Matching swimsuits and cover-ups for the daytime / water-toy energy. A "dressier" change for the late-afternoon reception-style hour. The indoor lounge handles the outfit transition without anyone having to disembark.

Decor. Minimal. Sashes for the bride and bridesmaids. Maid-of-honor-printed party-game cards. Banners if you must (taped to the aft deck wall, no adhesive on varnished surfaces). Matching plastic cups. No glass containers, no smoke machines, no large structural props.

Tech. Phones charged. Portable battery packs for the group (wind drains batteries fast). Waterproof phone pouches if using water toys. A Spotify playlist queued and ready, the boat's sound system pairs over Bluetooth.

Other. Sunscreen, towels (especially for water-toy charters), light layers for after sunset, sunglasses, hats in summer. Cash for the captain and crew tip (customary at 15–20% of the charter cost, not required, but always appreciated, and cash is the cleanest way to do it).
