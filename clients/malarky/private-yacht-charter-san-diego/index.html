What Private Means

What "private charter" actually means.

There are two ways to get on a boat in San Diego Bay.

A ticketed cruise sells individual seats on a 60 to 100 passenger boat. You board with thirty or fifty strangers, the route is fixed, the schedule is fixed, and the music is whatever the host has queued up. Around $55 per person, and it's a fine product if you just want to see the bay.

A private Malarky charter is the entire boat for your group. You and up to 11 guests, the full 47-foot catamaran, your route, your pace, your music. Want the captain to slow down for sunset photos at a particular angle? Done. Want to skip the loop and just sit in one quiet spot for an hour? That's fine. Bring your own champagne, your own food, your own playlist.

It costs more per group than buying individual tickets, but for a wedding, anniversary, milestone birthday, or any occasion where the day is supposed to be yours, that's the whole reason you'd do it.

Capacity

Up to 12 guests, U.S. Coast Guard certified.

Malarky's USCG capacity is twelve guests. That's the cap and we hold it firm, it's a Coast Guard rule, not a Malarky one.

For a private charter, twelve is the right number. Everyone has a seat, everyone has a view, and the conversations stay manageable. Larger groups on a similar-size boat end up clumped at one end while the rest of the vessel goes unused. At twelve, the whole boat is in use.

The Route

Your route, your pace.

San Diego Bay is sheltered, scenic, and varied enough that no two charters look the same even when they cover the same landmarks. Your captain will propose two or three route options at the start of the day based on the weather, the wind, the time of year, and what your group has told us you want from the day. You pick.

Sample landmarks that show up on most routes: the Coronado Bridge, the USS Midway moored at the Embarcadero, the Star of India sailing-ship museum, Seaport Village, the marinas at Shelter Island and Harbor Island, and the silhouette of Cabrillo National Monument on the western point. Most charters touch most of these. Your captain will adjust the order and pace to whatever your group is enjoying.

Wildlife sightings are part of the day, not a tour. Dolphin pods are common, they often pace the bow trampoline. Sea lions lounge on the channel buoys. Seabirds work the surface. In winter and early spring, gray whales and the occasional blue whale pass within sight of the bay's mouth. Seals are year-round residents. Your captain will point out anything notable as you go, but we don't run whale-watching tours and we don't advertise sightings as guarantees.

Anchor stops are part of most longer charters, thirty to ninety minutes at a sheltered spot for swimming, deploying paddleboards or the lily pad, or just floating in calm water. Sunset slots are popular for engagement and anniversary charters, and the captain will time the route around the light.

Every charter stays on San Diego Bay. That's intentional, the bay is the most charter-friendly water in the region: sheltered, scenic, landmark-rich, and active with wildlife. More time on the water, less transit, more of the day spent on the route that matters. We don't run open-Pacific or Mission Bay charters.

Included & Optional

What's included, what to bring, what to skip.

Every private charter on Malarky includes the same baseline package, captain, crew, the boat, the route. Add-ons are itemized. BYOB is welcome with two simple rules. Catering is on request via partner caterers if you'd rather not pack the food yourself.

  • USCG-licensed captain handling navigation, route, and anchoring, never an upcharge
  • Friendly crew for safety briefing, line handling, and on-board hospitality
  • BYOB & BYOF, bring your own beverages and food (no glass, no red wine)
  • Lifejackets for everyone aboard, sized for adults and kids
  • Onboard sound system that pairs with your phone for your own playlist
  • Flexible route within San Diego Bay, captain advises, your group decides
BYOB rules, briefly: no drinking from glass on deck (pour into the plastic cups we provide, safety, not style), and no red wine (it stains the upholstery and the deck). Beer, hard seltzer, champagne, white wine, cocktails in cans, and any non-glass food, all welcome.

Water-toy add-ons are optional, just let us know in advance: stand-up paddleboards, kayaks, a 15-foot lily pad floating platform, and tower jump pads. The lily pad is one of a kind on San Diego Bay. We'll have everything inflated and ready when you anchor.

If you'd rather not pack the food, we partner with a roster of San Diego caterers who handle anything from a charcuterie spread to a fully plated multi-course meal. Mention catering when you book and we'll send options that match your group, time of day, and any dietary needs.

What to bring: sunscreen, towels (especially if you're using the lily pad or paddleboards), and a light layer, the bay cools about 15 degrees once the sun drops. Sunglasses are an upgrade. Hats are smart in summer.
