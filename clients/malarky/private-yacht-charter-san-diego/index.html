What Private Means

What "private charter" actually means.

On Malarky, "private charter" is meant in the fullest sense. Your group books the entire 47-foot catamaran for the duration of your charter window. No other groups are sharing the boat. No one else is on the aft deck, the bow, the indoor lounge, or the swim platform. The whole vessel — every space — is yours.

That's a different product from the budget ticket model that's also sold on the bay. On a ticketed cruise, individual seats are sold to whoever buys them, and you board with thirty or fifty other people who also bought tickets. The boat sets a fixed schedule and a fixed route, the deck is shared, and the music is whoever-the-host-is's playlist. It's a fine product for what it is, but it's not a private charter in any meaningful sense.

The energy on a private charter is quieter and more your own. The pace is yours. The music is yours. Conversations don't have to be shouted over a tour guide narrating landmarks. If you want to anchor at a swim spot for an hour and skip the rest of the route, you can. If you want to slow down for sunset photos at a particular angle, the captain will reposition. The day is shaped around what you actually want to do.

Most guests who've experienced both models book private once and don't go back. The cost difference matters — private is more, no question — but for an occasion, the experience difference is decisive.

Capacity

Up to 12 guests, U.S. Coast Guard certified.

Malarky's USCG capacity is twelve guests — not thirteen, not "twelve plus a couple kids," not "fifteen if everyone's friendly." Twelve. We don't exceed it under any circumstances.

That cap is sometimes a constraint and sometimes a feature. For a private charter, twelve is genuinely a feature. Twelve people on a 47-foot platform means everyone has a seat, everyone has a view, and the conversations stay manageable. Larger groups on a similar-size boat end up clumped at one end while the rest of the vessel goes unused. At twelve, the whole boat is in motion.

The Route

Your route, your pace.

San Diego Bay is sheltered, scenic, and varied enough that no two charters look the same even when they cover the same landmarks. Your captain will propose two or three route options at the start of the day based on the weather, the wind, the time of year, and what your group has told us you want from the day. You pick.

Sample landmarks that show up on most routes: the Coronado Bridge, the USS Midway moored at the Embarcadero, the Star of India sailing-ship museum, Seaport Village, the marinas at Shelter Island and Harbor Island, and the silhouette of Cabrillo National Monument on the western point. Most charters touch most of these. Your captain will adjust the order and pace to whatever your group is enjoying.

Wildlife sightings are part of the day, not a tour. Dolphin pods are common — they often pace the bow trampoline. Sea lions lounge on the channel buoys. Seabirds work the surface. In winter and early spring, gray whales and the occasional blue whale pass within sight of the bay's mouth. Seals are year-round residents. Your captain will point out anything notable as you go, but we don't run whale-watching tours and we don't advertise sightings as guarantees.

Anchor stops are part of most longer charters — thirty to ninety minutes at a sheltered spot for swimming, deploying paddleboards or the lily pad, or just floating in calm water. Sunset slots are popular for engagement and anniversary charters, and the captain will time the route around the light.

One important note: we operate exclusively in San Diego Bay. We do not run open-Pacific charters and we do not operate in Mission Bay.

Included & Optional

What's included, what to bring, what to skip.

Every private charter on Malarky includes the same baseline package — captain, crew, the boat, the route. Add-ons are itemized. BYOB is welcome with two simple rules. Catering is on request via partner caterers if you'd rather not pack the food yourself.

  • USCG-licensed captain handling navigation, route, and anchoring — never an upcharge
  • Friendly crew for safety briefing, line handling, and on-board hospitality
  • BYOB & BYOF — bring your own beverages and food (no glass, no red wine)
  • Lifejackets for everyone aboard, sized for adults and kids
  • Onboard sound system that pairs with your phone for your own playlist
  • Flexible route within San Diego Bay — captain advises, your group decides
BYOB rules, briefly: no glass containers (any liquid in glass gets transferred to plastic cups by our crew before boarding — safety, not style), and no red wine (it stains the upholstery and the deck). Beer, hard seltzer, champagne, white wine, cocktails in cans, and any non-glass food — all welcome.

Water-toy add-ons are optional and confirmed in advance: stand-up paddleboards, kayaks, a 16-foot lily pad floating mat, tower jump pads, and an 11-foot inflatable waterslide off the bow. The waterslide is the unique one in the San Diego charter fleet — let us know which add-ons you want and we'll have everything inflated and ready when you anchor.

Catering on request — we partner with a roster of San Diego caterers and can arrange anything from a charcuterie spread to a fully plated multi-course meal. Mention catering when you book and we'll send menu and pricing options that match your group, time of day, and any dietary needs.

What guests should bring: sunscreen, towels (especially if you're using the waterslide or paddleboards), and a light layer — the bay can cool fifteen degrees once the sun drops, especially on a sunset charter. Sunglasses are a real upgrade. Hats are smart in summer.

Pricing & how to book.

Private charters on Malarky are quote-based. Pricing varies by season, time of day (sunset slots run higher), group size, and any add-ons you'd like included. We'll send a clean quote within a few hours of your inquiry — no surprises, no padding.

Booking minimums and cancellation

The minimum charter length is two hours. Most groups book three to four hours, which is what we recommend — long enough to take in the bay at a reasonable pace, anchor for a swim, deploy the water-toy add-ons, eat and drink, and return without rushing. Anything shorter feels compressed by the time you've boarded and gotten settled.
